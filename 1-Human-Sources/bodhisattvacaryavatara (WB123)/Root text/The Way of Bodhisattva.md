## 1. Chapter 1

With deep respect, I bow to the Buddhas who possess the dharmakaya, 
To their spiritual heirs, and to all who are worthy of reverence. 
I will now briefly explain how to enter the discipline of the Bodhisattvas, 
The heirs of the Buddhas, in accordance with the scriptures. ^1-1

I haven't written anything new or original here. 
I don't possess the skill of elegant composition. 
I didn't create this with the intention of benefiting others. 
I wrote this simply to cultivate these teachings in my own mind. ^1-2

May the power of my faith increase for now through these efforts to cultivate virtue. 
And if others who share my aspirations happen to see these teachings, may their encounter be meaningful and beneficial. ^1-3

This precious human life with its freedoms and advantages is extremely rare to obtain. 
Since we have gained this opportunity to fulfill meaningful human goals, 
if we fail to accomplish what is beneficial in this lifetime, 
how could we expect to obtain such a perfect opportunity again in the future? ^1-4

Just as a flash of lightning briefly illuminates the darkness on a cloudy night, 
so too, by the power of the Buddha, 
moments of merit and wisdom occasionally appear in the world. ^1-5

Therefore, our positive actions are always feeble, 
while our negative actions are powerful and truly unbearable. Nothing except the mind of perfect awakening can overcome such negativity—no other virtue has this power. ^1-6

After contemplating deeply for many eons, 
the mighty sages have seen that this alone—bodhicitta—brings true benefit. 
Through this practice, countless beings can easily attain the highest happiness. The supreme buddhas, who have reflected profoundly across many ages, recognized that this mind of awakening is what genuinely helps all beings. Through bodhicitta, immeasurable multitudes of people can achieve ultimate happiness with remarkable ease. ^1-7

If you wish to overcome the hundreds of sufferings of existence, 
If you want to remove the unhappiness of living beings, 
And if you desire to experience countless forms of happiness, 
Never abandon the awakening mind. ^1-8

The moment the awakening mind arises within you, 
those who were wretched and bound in the prison of samsara will be called "children of the Buddhas," 
and will become worthy of reverence from both gods and humans throughout the world. ^1-9

Like the supreme elixir that transforms base metals into gold, 
This awakening mind takes our impure bodies 
And transforms them into the priceless form of a Buddha. 
Therefore, hold firmly to this precious bodhicitta. ^1-10

After careful and thorough examination with boundless wisdom, 
The sole captain of all beings found this to be most precious. 
Those who wish to be free from the suffering of existence 
Should firmly hold onto the precious awakening mind. ^1-11

All other virtuous actions are like banana trees— 
They bear fruit once and then are depleted. 
But the tree of awakening mind continually 
Produces fruit without exhaustion, growing ever more abundant. ^1-12

Even if you have committed terrible wrongdoing, 
You can be quickly freed by relying on this awakening mind, 
Just as someone escapes great danger by depending on a brave protector. Why then would thoughtful people not rely on it? ^1-13

Like the fire at the end of time, 
bodhicitta completely burns away even the greatest negative actions in a single moment. 
The wise and compassionate protector Maitreya explained its immeasurable benefits to Sudhana. ^1-14

In brief, the awakening mind should be understood as having two aspects: 
the mind that aspires to enlightenment and the mind that actively engages in the path to enlightenment. ^1-15

Just as we understand the difference between wanting to go somewhere and actually going there, in the same way, the wise should understand the distinction between these two states.

21

From the aspiring mind of awakening, great results emerge while still in the cycle of existence. However, unlike the engaged mind of awakening, it does not produce a continuous stream of merit.

22

From the moment one genuinely adopts this unwavering resolve to free the limitless realm of beings, this steadfast commitment to liberate all living creatures without exception—this is when one truly takes up the awakening mind.

23

From this moment on, whether I am asleep or even distracted, a powerful and continuous stream of merit will flow forth abundantly, equal to the vastness of space.

24

This teaching was logically explained in the Questions of Suvishuddhamati (Good Minded). Even for those of lesser capacity, the Buddha himself taught it for the benefit of all beings.

25

Even if you merely think, "I will relieve the headaches of living beings," this intention to help others brings immeasurable merit. So imagine the merit of actually doing so.

26

Wishing to remove the countless sufferings Of each and every living being, And desiring to help each one achieve Limitless qualities—what more needs to be said?

27

What father or mother has ever had such a caring attitude? What god, sage, or even Brahma himself has ever held such concern for others?

28

If these beings have never even dreamed of such an attitude for their own sake, how could they possibly develop it for the sake of others?

29

A precious and extraordinary state of mind has emerged—something truly marvelous that never existed before: the thought of benefiting all beings, which doesn't arise even for people's own self-interest.

30

How can we possibly measure the merit of the precious awakening mind? It is the source of happiness for all beings, and transforms the suffering of all creatures into something meaningful.

31

Even just sincerely wishing to help others Surpasses the merit of making offerings to buddhas. So what need is there to mention The merit of actively working for the happiness of all beings?

32

Though we all wish to be free from suffering, we ironically rush straight toward the very things that cause our pain. And while we desperately want happiness, in our confusion we destroy our own well-being as if it were our enemy.

33

For those who are deprived of happiness And burdened with many sufferings, May I bring complete fulfillment through all kinds of joy And cut away all their pain and distress.

34

What can compare to this virtue that dispels ignorance? Where can we find a friend equal to this? Where can we find merit like this?

35

If someone who repays kindness with kindness Is worthy of praise, Then what need we say of bodhisattvas Who do good without being asked?

36

Even someone who provides a simple meal to a small number of people, Or offers just a moment's worth of food, Or feeds the hungry for half a day, even disrespectfully— People honor them and say, "This person is doing good."

37

Even considering in your mind the wish to give supreme happiness—like that of the Buddhas—to countless beings for a long time, fulfilling all their desires, is tremendously beneficial. What need is there to mention actually giving such gifts continuously?

38

If someone develops ill will toward a Bodhisattva who is their benefactor, the Buddha taught that they will remain in hell realms for as many eons as the number of moments they harbored that ill will.

39

When someone cultivates genuine joy in their mind, the results grow even more abundantly. For the children of the buddhas, even under great pressure, negative actions do not arise, while virtuous qualities naturally increase. If someone develops sincere appreciation and pure faith, the beneficial results will multiply even further. For bodhisattvas, even in the most challenging situations, harmful actions do not occur, and positive qualities flourish effortlessly.

40

I bow to the bodies of those in whom this precious, supreme mind has arisen. I take refuge in that source of happiness which brings joy even to those who harm it.

In order to firmly grasp this precious mind of awakening, I will make excellent offerings to the Tathagatas, To the sacred Dharma, to the spotless Three Jewels, And to the children of the Buddha, those oceans of good qualities.

43

All the flowers and fruits that exist in the world, All the various types of medicines that can be found, All the precious treasures throughout the world, And all the pure, refreshing waters that exist;

44

Like precious mountains, and similarly, Secluded places in forests that are peaceful and delightful, Trees adorned and decorated with flowers, And trees with excellent fruit weighing down their branches.

45

In the world of gods and others, there are fragrances, incense, wish-fulfilling trees, and precious trees. There are also naturally growing crops that need no planting, and various other suitable ornaments and offerings.

46

Lakes and ponds adorned with lotus flowers, Beautifully inhabited by sweet-voiced, enchanting swans, And the vast expanse of space that reaches the limits of all realms— All of these cannot be truly possessed by anyone.

47

Having embraced this wisdom with my mind, I now offer it sincerely to the supreme Buddha and all his spiritual heirs. May these most worthy recipients, filled with great compassion, look upon me with kindness and accept this offering of mine.

48

I am a poor person without merit, and I have no other wealth to offer as a gift. Therefore, Protector who cares for the welfare of others, please accept this through your own power, for my benefit.

49

I offer my entire being forever to the Buddhas and their heirs. Supreme ones, please accept me completely. With deep respect, I place myself in your service.

50

Since I am completely embraced by you, I will live without fear in the world and work for the benefit of all beings. I will fully overcome my past negative actions and never commit harmful deeds again.

51

The bathing chamber is filled with delightful fragrance, with rows of crystal floors that shine brilliantly clear. It has beautiful pillars adorned with glowing jewels, and overhead hangs a canopy shimmering with the lustrous light of pearls.

52

I offer a bath to the Buddhas and their spiritual heirs With many precious vases filled with fragrant, pleasing waters, Accompanied by beautiful songs and music.

53

They wiped the Buddha's body with immaculately clean cloths perfumed with the finest fragrances. Then they offered the Buddha exquisite robes of beautiful colors that were supremely fragrant and of the highest quality.

54

I will adorn Noble Samantabhadra, Manjushri, Avalokiteshvara, and others with various fine, soft, delicate fabrics and hundreds of excellent ornaments.

55

I will anoint the bodies of all the mighty Buddhas with the finest fragrances, whose sweet scents permeate throughout the billion-world system. Their bodies, radiant and gleaming like refined, polished gold, will shine with brilliant light.

56

I offer to the supreme worthy one, the mighty sage, these beautiful flowers—mandarava blossoms, lotuses, blue water lilies, and all other sweet-scented blooms—along with lovely, well-arranged garlands that delight the mind.

57

I offer clouds of incense, filled with captivating fragrances of the finest perfumes that spread in all directions. I also offer divine food with various delicious things to eat and drink.

58

I offer these brilliant golden lotus lamps, radiant with light. Upon this prepared ground, anointed with perfumed incense, I scatter beautiful, delightful flower petals.

59

I offer to those with compassionate nature These immeasurable palaces filled with pleasing songs of praise, Beautifully adorned with hanging pearl and jewel ornaments, All shining brilliantly as decorations of boundless space.

60

A beautiful jeweled parasol with a golden handle, Encircled with lovely ornamental designs, Gracefully shaped and pleasing to behold when raised— I offer this continually to the mighty sages.

61

May there also be clouds of offerings— Pleasant music, beautiful melodies, and delightful sounds— Remaining present everywhere To satisfy and relieve the suffering of all beings.

62

May a constant shower of precious gems, flowers, and other offerings Fall without interruption upon all the sacred Dharma, Upon all stupas and sacred images, And upon all that is worthy of veneration.

63

Just as Manjushri and the other bodhisattvas made offerings to the victorious Buddhas, in the same way, I too make offerings to the Tathagatas, the protectors, together with their spiritual heirs.

64

I praise those who possess oceans of good qualities with an ocean of verses of praise. May these melodious clouds of praise definitely spread everywhere.

65

I bow down with as many bodies as there are atoms in all universes, Paying homage to all the Buddhas of the three times, Along with the sublime Dharma And the excellent Assembly.

66

I bow to the foundations of the awakening mind, And I pay homage to the sacred shrines. I offer respect to the preceptors and teachers, And I prostrate to those of supreme spiritual discipline.

67

Until I reach the heart of awakening, I take refuge in the Buddhas. Likewise, I take refuge in the Dharma and in the assembly of Bodhisattvas.

68

To all the fully awakened Buddhas and bodhisattvas dwelling in every direction, those who possess great compassion, I join my palms together and make this request.

69

From beginningless time in this cycle of existence, whether in this life or in past lives, I have committed harmful actions without understanding, or have encouraged others to do the same.

70

Overcome by ignorance and confusion, Whatever harmful acts I have rejoiced in, I now recognize these mistakes And sincerely confess them to you, my protector.

71

Whatever harm I have done through my body, speech, and mind— driven by disturbing emotions— toward the Three Jewels, my parents, my teachers, or others, I acknowledge it all.

72

I, a wrongdoer stained by many faults, Confess to all the guides Every terrible negative action That I have committed.

73

I may die before my negative actions are purified. Please protect me quickly and surely, So I may be freed from this burden— Show me the path to swift liberation.

74

Death is untrustworthy and cannot be relied upon. It won't wait for what you have or haven't done. Whether you're sick or healthy, no one can depend on how long they'll live, as death comes suddenly and without warning.

75

I did not understand that we must leave everything behind when we depart this life. Because of this ignorance, I committed all kinds of harmful actions for the sake of friends and enemies alike.

76

Enemies will cease to exist, Friends will also disappear, I too will pass away, And likewise, everything will vanish.

77

Like experiences in a dream, whatever things we've enjoyed become mere objects of memory. Once they've passed, we can no longer see any of them.

78

Even in this short lifetime, many friends and enemies have come and gone. The negative actions I committed for their sake—actions too terrible to bear—now stand right before me.

79

Because I failed to realize that I am impermanent and temporary, I have committed many harmful actions driven by ignorance, attachment, and anger.

80

Day and night pass without pause, And this life constantly diminishes. With no way to add more time, How could I possibly avoid death?

81

Even as I lie on my deathbed, surrounded by all my family and friends, I alone must experience the sensations of my life ending.

82

When you're seized by Death's messengers, What good are relatives? What good are friends? At that moment, only merit can protect you, And even that I have failed to cultivate.

83

Protector, due to my carelessness, I have failed to recognize these dangers ahead. For the sake of this temporary, impermanent life, I have committed numerous harmful deeds.

84

When people are led to the place of execution, where their limbs will be cut off, they become terrified. Their mouths go dry, the blood drains from their eyes, and their entire appearance changes dramatically from how they looked before.

85

When seized by the terrifying messengers of death with their frightening appearance, and struck down by the great fear of illness, one becomes utterly miserable—what more needs to be said?

86

"Who will protect me well from this great fear?" With panicked eyes wide open, I search desperately in all four directions for refuge.

87

Looking in all four directions and finding no shelter, they become completely discouraged. If there is no refuge in that place, what will I do then?

88

Therefore, I take refuge now, from this very day, In the victorious ones, protectors of all beings, Who strive diligently to shelter all creatures, Those mighty ones who dispel all fears.

89

The teachings that he fully comprehended Dispel the fears of cyclic existence. I also take genuine refuge In the community of bodhisattvas.

90

Terrified and overcome with fear, I offer myself completely to Samantabhadra. To Manjushri as well, I freely give my body.

91

With compassion that never fails, To that protector Avalokiteshvara, I cry out with the voice of desperation: Please protect me, a bearer of wrongdoing.

92

I desperately cry out from the depths of my heart, seeking refuge in the noble Essence of Space, the Essence of Earth, and all the great compassionate protectors.

93

Seeing the one before whom the messengers of death And other hostile forces flee in all four directions in terror, I take refuge in the Vajra Holder.

94

In the past, I disregarded your teachings. Now, having seen great dangers, I come to you for refuge. Please quickly remove my fears.

95

Even if we're frightened by common illnesses and must follow a doctor's instructions, what need is there to mention that we should follow the Buddha's guidance when we're constantly afflicted by the diseases of desire, hatred, and hundreds of other mental afflictions?

96

If even one person could destroy all the people living on earth, and no medicine to heal them could be found anywhere in the world...

97

When the all-knowing physician offers instructions that can remove every source of pain, those who think about it but fail to follow this advice are extremely foolish and deserve criticism.

98

If we must be careful even near small, ordinary dangers, How much more so with deep chasms that drop a thousand miles, Where a fall would mean suffering for a very long time?

99

It is unwise to be complacent, thinking, "I certainly won't die today." The time when I will cease to exist will inevitably come.

100

Who has given me this fearlessness? How can I be certain to escape from this? If my deliverance is not absolutely guaranteed, how can my mind remain at ease?

101

What more do I have left than those pleasures I've already experienced and lost? Yet I became so attached to them that I disobeyed my teacher's instructions.

102

In this life and likewise after, When I must leave behind all family and friends, And journey onward all alone, What difference does it make who loves me or who doesn't?

103

Suffering arises from harmful actions. How can I truly free myself from this? Day and night, without pause, I should reflect on this question alone.

104

Due to my ignorance and confusion, I have committed various wrongdoings—either those that are naturally harmful or those prohibited by the teachings.

105

In the direct presence of the Protector, With joined palms and a mind fearful of suffering, I bow down again and again And confess all my negative actions.

106

I ask all the guides to recognize my wrongdoings and mistakes as they truly are. Since these actions were not good, I promise never to commit them again.

I freely give my body, all my possessions, and every virtue I have created in the past, present, and future—holding nothing back—to fulfill the needs of all living beings.

118

By letting go of everything, one reaches liberation. My mind is set on achieving this freedom. Since I must surrender everything at once, the supreme gift is to give to all living beings.

119

I have freely given this body of mine to all living beings. They may do whatever they please with it - kill me, insult me, beat me, or anything else they wish. Let them use it however they want.

120

Even if others play with my body, mock me, or make me an object of ridicule, I have already given this body away. So why should I worry about what happens to it?

121

Let them do whatever actions that do not harm that person. And may no one who encounters me ever be left without purpose.

122

123

124

I will be a protector for those without protection, A guide for those on the path, A boat, a raft, and a bridge For all who wish to cross over.

125

For those who seek a sanctuary, may I be their refuge; For those who need a place to rest, may I be their shelter; As for myself, I wish to be a servant to all beings— May I become a faithful servant to everyone in need.

126

Like a wish-fulfilling jewel, a perfect vase, a powerful spell, a healing elixir, or a magical wish-granting tree—bodhicitta becomes the source that fulfills all the desires of living beings.

127

Like the earth and other great elements, Like the sky itself, may I always be A fundamental support for the countless beings, Sustaining their many and diverse ways of life.

128

May I be a source of sustenance for all beings Throughout the endless reaches of space, In every way possible, Until they all attain liberation.

129

Just as the enlightened ones of the past First awakened the mind of enlightenment And then progressively trained In the bodhisattva practices,

130

Just as the buddhas have done, I too will generate the awakening mind for the benefit of all beings. And likewise, I will train myself step by step in the precepts of this path.

131

In this way, those with wisdom should firmly embrace the awakening mind. And to further develop it more fully, they should encourage their mind with these inspiring words.

132

Today my life has become fruitful. Having obtained a human existence, I have now been born into the Buddha's family. Today I have become a child of the Buddhas.

133

From now on, I will make every effort to act in ways that honor this lineage. I will behave in a manner that is pure and dignified, never bringing disgrace to this noble family.

134

Just as a blind person might find a precious jewel in a pile of garbage, in the same way, through some fortunate chance, this awakening mind has arisen within me.

135

This is the supreme medicine that conquers death itself for all beings. This is also the inexhaustible treasure that removes the poverty of all living beings.

136

This is the supreme medicine that heals the deepest illnesses of all beings. It is also the shade tree where weary travelers can rest after wandering exhausted along the roads of existence.

137

It is a shared vessel that rescues all beings from the lower realms. It is like the rising moon of the mind that soothes the burning suffering of all beings' afflictions.

138

The great sun that completely removes The darkness of ignorance from living beings— This is the essential butter Churned from the milk of the sacred Dharma.

139

For travelers journeying along the paths of existence Who wish to experience happiness and enjoyment, This bodhicitta serves as the supreme source of joy, Satisfying the great guests who are all sentient beings.

140

Today, in the presence of all protectors, I invite all beings to buddhahood and to happiness along the way. Gods, demigods, and all others—rejoice!

142

The children of the Buddha should firmly hold onto the awakening mind in this way. Without laziness, they should always strive to never transgress the training.

143

Whatever was started impulsively or without careful examination, even if you've made a commitment to it, should be reconsidered: "Should I do this or let it go?"

144

What the Buddhas and their spiritual heirs have examined with their great wisdom, and what I myself have thoroughly investigated—what reason could there be to delay following this path?

145

If I make such a promise, yet fail to fulfill it through my actions, I will have deceived all these beings. What will become of my future journey?

146

Even if someone merely thinks about giving away a small, ordinary thing, but then doesn't actually give it—even this person is said to be reborn as a hungry ghost.

147

If someone sincerely seeks the highest happiness, yet deliberately deceives all beings along the way, how could they possibly reach a fortunate rebirth?

148

Even those who have abandoned the awakening mind will still be liberated, for the workings of karma are inconceivable and known only to the all-knowing ones.

149

For a bodhisattva, this is considered the most serious downfall. If this happens, it diminishes one's ability to benefit all beings.

150

Anyone who, even for a single moment, blocks or obstructs the merit of this bodhicitta will experience endless suffering in lower realms, because they have diminished the welfare of sentient beings.

151

If destroying the happiness of even one being Leads to my own downfall, What need is there to mention what happens When I harm the happiness of countless beings throughout limitless space?

152

When powerful negative actions and the powerful awakening mind alternate in one's life, progress through the levels of awakening is greatly delayed.

153

Therefore, I will diligently fulfill my commitment with respect. If I don't make an effort starting today, I will only sink lower and lower.

154

Though countless Buddhas who work for the benefit of all beings have come and gone, because of my own faults, I have not become the object of their healing care.

155

If I continue to act in the same harmful way, the same consequences will follow again and again. I'll experience suffering in lower realms, illness, imprisonment, being cut, slashed, and other similar pains.

156

The appearance of a Buddha, finding faith, obtaining a human life, and having the capacity to practice virtue—these are all so rare. When will such opportunities come together again?

157

Though we may have this day of good health, Food to eat, and freedom from harm, Life deceives in a single moment, And our bodies are merely on temporary loan.

158

With actions like mine, I won't even obtain a human body again. And without a human body, I will only create negative karma, not positive.

159

When I have the good fortune and opportunity to practice virtue, yet I fail to do so myself, what will become of me when I'm lost and suffering in the lower realms? What can I possibly do then?

160

If you neither perform virtuous actions nor accumulate negative deeds, even after billions of eons, you will not even hear the term "higher rebirth."

161

For this very reason, the Blessed One taught that obtaining a human birth is as rare as a turtle's neck finding the hole in a yoke floating on the vast ocean. Human existence is extremely difficult to attain.

162

If negative actions done in just a single moment Can lead to aeons of suffering in the lowest hell realms, Then given the harmful deeds we've accumulated Since beginningless time in this cycle of existence, How could we possibly expect to reach the higher realms?

163

After experiencing just those consequences, one will not be completely free, because even while suffering the results of past misdeeds, new negative actions continue to arise.

164

Having found such freedom and opportunity, if I fail to practice virtue, there could be no greater deception than this, no greater foolishness than this.

165

If, having understood this truth, I still become lazy out of confusion, then when the time of death arrives, I will experience great anguish and regret.

166

If the unbearable fires of hell scorch my body for a long time, my mind will certainly be tormented by the blazing flames of unbearable regret.

167

Having somehow found this precious opportunity that is so difficult to obtain—this ground of benefit—if I, despite having awareness and understanding, allow myself to be led back to those hellish states once again...

168

Like someone bewildered by a spell, I seem to have lost my mind in this situation. I don't even understand why I'm so confused—what is happening inside me?

169

Enemies like anger and craving have no arms or legs, nor are they brave or clever. So how have they managed to enslave me like this?

170

When someone deliberately harms me while living in my own home—taking pleasure in causing me pain—if I do not become angry and instead practice patience toward them, this is misplaced tolerance that deserves criticism.

171

Even if all the gods and demigods were to rise up against me as enemies, they still wouldn't be able to lead me into the fires of the deepest hell.

172

These powerful enemies - my disturbing emotions - Would destroy even Mount Meru completely, leaving not even ashes behind, Yet in a single moment, I can cast them aside.

173

My inner afflictions—these enemies of mine—have persisted seemingly without beginning or end. Unlike them, all other enemies cannot endure for such a long time.

174

When we cultivate and follow practices that align with harmony, everyone experiences benefit and happiness. But when we indulge our disturbing emotions, they only bring more suffering and harm in return.

175

If this enemy, this self-cherishing that has been my constant adversary for such a long time—the single cause that multiplies all forms of harm—firmly establishes itself within my heart, how could I possibly feel fearless joy within this cycle of existence?

176

The guards of samsara's prison, who become executioners in hell and other realms—if my mind remains caught in the net of attachment to these things, how can I possibly find happiness?

177

As long as I have not directly and completely defeated this enemy, I will not abandon my efforts here. Even those with fierce pride who become angry at the slightest harm will not rest until they have overcome their opponent. In the same way, until I have utterly vanquished this adversary of mine face to face, I will not give up my perseverance in this matter. Even proud people who become enraged at minor offenses will not sleep until they have conquered their foe.

178

When facing the disturbing emotions that cause us suffering and lead to death, Those who wish to defeat them on the battlefield should be determined. Just as a warrior endures the pain of arrows and spears, And doesn't retreat or flee until the goal is accomplished.

179

Today, I strive to defeat what is certainly my natural enemy—that which always causes all my suffering. Why even mention that I won't become discouraged or disheartened by the hundreds of sufferings I might encounter along the way?

180

If we can wear the scars inflicted by pointless enemies as if they were ornaments on our body, then why should suffering hinder me when I am genuinely striving for a great purpose?

181

Even fishermen, butchers, farmers, and others who are concerned only with their own livelihoods endure hardships like cold and heat. So why shouldn't I also endure such difficulties for the sake of bringing happiness to all beings?

182

When I have made a solemn promise to free All beings throughout space, to its farthest reaches, From their disturbing emotions, While I myself have not yet freed myself from my own disturbing emotions.

183

Without even knowing my own limits, how can I speak without seeming crazy? Despite this, I will never turn back from fighting against my negative emotions.

184

I should embrace this challenge and engage fully in this battle, confronting these disturbing emotions directly. For these kinds of afflictive states can only be overcome by the forces that conquer such disturbances.

185

I would rather be burned alive or killed, even have my head cut off, than ever bow down to the enemy of afflictive emotions.

186

When ordinary enemies are driven from your land, they may settle elsewhere, gather strength, and return to attack again. But the enemy of disturbing emotions works differently.

187

When we remove disturbing emotions from our mind using the eye of wisdom, where do they go? Where do they remain, waiting to return and harm us again? They have no independent existence—they simply disappear when we stop fixating on them with our attachment.

188

Disturbing emotions do not reside in objects, nor in the sense organs, nor somewhere in between, nor elsewhere. So where do they exist, these things that cause harm to all beings? If there is no self, then the connection between actions and their results makes no sense. After all, once an action is performed and ceases to exist, whose action would it be?

772

Actions and their results are distinct, yet they occur without a separate self as their agent. Since both parties already agree on this point, isn't this debate pointless?

773

It is impossible to actually see what we call "cause having its effect." Rather, we simply label "doer" and "experiencer" in reference to a single continuum.

774

The mind of the past and the mind of the future are not the self, since they don't actually exist. And if we say the present mind that has arisen is the self, then when that mind ceases, the self would also disappear.

775

Like a plantain tree that, when split apart piece by piece, has nothing solid inside, similarly, when we analyze ourselves through careful examination, we find no real, inherent self.

776

If you say, "If sentient beings don't truly exist, then who is there to show compassion to?" The answer is: We accept these beings—who are merely imagined through delusion—for the purpose of achieving our spiritual goal.

777

If beings don't exist, who will receive the benefit? True, but this is accepted for the sake of ending confusion. Even though beings may not ultimately exist, we don't reject the confused idea of 'benefitting others' when our purpose is to relieve suffering.

778

The pride that causes suffering Grows stronger through confusion about the self. If you ask, "How can I prevent this?" The answer is: meditation on selflessness is supreme.

779

The body is not the feet or lower legs. The thighs and waist are not the body either. The stomach and back are not the body, nor are the chest and shoulders the body.

780

The ribs and sides are not the body. The armpits and shoulders are not the body. Neither are the internal organs the body. If the head and neck are also not the body, then what exactly is this body?

781

If each part of this body exists only in relation to other parts, and each component exists only in relation to its own components, then where does the body itself actually exist?

782

If the self resided in all parts of the body—hands and so forth—then there would be just as many separate selves as there are body parts.

783

If the body doesn't exist either inside or outside itself, how can the body exist in parts like hands and so on? And if the body is nothing more than these parts like hands, then how can we say it truly exists at all?

784

Thus, through confusion, we come to think of our hands and other parts—which are not themselves the body—as "the body." This is like how we mistake a scarecrow for a person simply because of its particular shape and arrangement.

785

As long as all the contributing conditions remain together, the body appears as if it were a person. Similarly, as long as the hands and other parts are present, they continue to appear as a body.

786

Just as a hand is simply a collection of fingers, and each finger is itself just a collection of joints, so too are the joints themselves divisible into their own parts.

787

When divided into its parts, it becomes particles. These particles themselves can be separated into directional parts. And these directional parts, being without discrete parts, Are like empty space—thus particles themselves do not truly exist.

788

Who with good sense would become attached to forms that are like a dream? When the body doesn't truly exist, who is male and who is female?

789

If suffering truly exists, why doesn't it destroy joy? And if pleasure truly exists, why don't delicious foods bring happiness to those consumed by grief and pain?

790

If something powerful overwhelms experience, but that experience isn't actually felt, how can it be called a sensation? What isn't personally experienced cannot truly be considered a feeling.

791

Even in what we call "pleasure," subtle suffering exists. Isn't it true that what we call pleasure is merely the removal of gross suffering? If someone objects, "No, pleasure is something distinct from the absence of suffering," I would reply that even this so-called distinct pleasure is itself just another form of suffering, only more subtle.

792

If suffering does not arise when challenging conditions appear, doesn't this prove that our experience of pain is simply the result of our own mental fixation and attachment to sensations?

793

For this very reason, cultivate this antidote: discerning awareness. This contemplative analysis is the nourishment of meditators and yogis who have emerged from the field of thorough investigation.

794

If the sense faculty and its object have something between them, how could they ever meet? And if there is nothing between them, they would be one and the same—so how could one thing meet with itself?

795

Tiny particles cannot enter other tiny particles. This is because they have no space and are of equal size. What does not enter cannot mix, and what does not mix cannot make contact.

796

How can we reasonably speak of "contact" with something that has no parts? If you can see how something without parts could make contact with anything, please explain it.

797

Consciousness has no physical form, so it cannot truly make contact with objects. Furthermore, the idea of consciousness as a collection or composite has no real substance - just as we examined earlier.

798

If there is no actual contact, how can feelings arise? What is the purpose of this exhausting effort? Who can harm what?

799

When you realize there is no one who feels And no actual feeling itself, Seeing this reality clearly, Why wouldn't craving naturally fade away?

800

What we see or touch is like a dream or illusion. Because sensations arise together with the mind, they are not actually seen by that experience itself.

801

Past and future lives cannot be directly experienced, only remembered. The self cannot truly experience itself, nor can it be experienced by others.

802

There is no actual "feeler" of sensations. Therefore, the sensations themselves are not a self. In this way, when there is no self among these aggregates, why would anyone harm this collection?

803

The mind does not reside in the sense faculties. It is not in physical forms or other objects, nor is it somewhere in between. It is neither inside as the mind itself, nor outside, nor can it be found anywhere else.

804

What is neither the body nor separate from it, neither mixed with it nor apart from it anywhere—this is nothing at all. Therefore, the true nature of living beings is nirvana.

805

If awareness exists before the object to be known, what does it focus on to arise? If awareness and its object exist simultaneously, what does awareness focus on to arise?

806

If the objects of knowledge existed later, what would give rise to awareness of them? In this way, it is impossible to truly understand the arising of all phenomena.

807

If conventional reality doesn't exist in this way, then how could the two truths exist? And if this conventional reality depends on something else, how could sentient beings ever reach liberation?

808

These are just the concepts in others' minds, not one's own relative truth. If later confirmed, then it exists. If not, relative truth itself would not exist.

809

The thinker and what is thought about depend on each other. Just as we rely on common understanding when explaining all our reasoned investigations.

810

When analytical insight is used to examine itself, that very analysis becomes the subject of further analysis. Thus, the process of investigation has no end.

811

When you thoroughly examine what can be examined, you find no true foundation for analytical thought. Since there is no foundation, nothing truly arises. This state is what we call nirvana.

812

For anyone who thinks both of these are true, they're in an extremely difficult position. If something exists because we perceive it, then what supports the existence of that perception itself?

813

If knowledge arises from the objects of knowledge, then what supports these objects of knowledge in the first place? If you say they exist in mutual dependence on each other, then neither truly exists at all.

814

If there is no child, one cannot be a parent. But where does the child come from? Without a child, there is no parent; likewise, neither exists independently of the other.

815

Just as a sprout grows from a seed and that seed can be understood through the sprout, why can't we understand the existence of objects through the knowledge arising from them?

816

If we know a seed exists because we can see its sprout, then how do we know that our awareness itself exists? What proves the existence of the consciousness that understands objects of knowledge?

817

In everyday experience, we can directly observe all kinds of causes. The different varieties of lotus stems and similar things are produced by different types of causes.

818

If you ask, "What causes this to happen?" It comes from previous causes. Why can causes produce effects? Because of the power inherent in previous causes.

819

If you claim that Ishvara (a supreme deity) is the cause of all beings, then please first explain: what exactly is this "Ishvara"? If you say it's the elements, well, that's fine—but why exhaust yourself arguing over mere names?

820

However, the earth and other elements are many in number, impermanent yet unmoving, not divine beings, something to be trodden upon, and impure. Therefore, these cannot possibly be the Supreme Lord.

821

Since divine powers lack permanence and stability, and since we've already refuted the concept of a self, what's the point of discussing an unconscious creator? Talking about an unconscious creator accomplishes nothing.

822

If desires arise from those causes, then what exactly produces desire? Aren't I, along with earth and other elements, and even the divine essence, all impermanent? And doesn't consciousness arise from the objects of knowledge?

823

Since beginningless time, happiness and suffering have been created by our actions. Think about it: what is there that isn't produced by karma? If causes have no beginning, how could their results have a beginning?

824

Why doesn't it always act? It does not depend on anything else. If there is nothing else that causes it and nothing else exists, what could it possibly depend on?

825

If we examine the matter, it's the combination of causes and conditions that produces results, not some divine creator. Results cannot arise from an incomplete collection of causes, nor can they arise without the necessary causes being present. Nothing has the power to arise independently of its causes.

826

If a divine creator acts unwillingly, then he must be under someone else's control. If he acts according to his desires, then he's dependent on those desires. And if he's compelled to act, how can he truly be all-powerful?

827

Even those who claim that atoms are permanent have already been refuted earlier. The Samkhyas believe that an eternal principal force is the cause of all living beings.

828

When the qualities known as courage, energy, and darkness exist in balance, this is called the "principal state." When they are not in balance, this is said to lead to wandering in samsara.

829

It is not reasonable for one thing to have three inherent natures. Therefore, such a thing cannot exist. Similarly, qualities cannot exist inherently, because they too would be subject to the same threefold analysis.

830

Without good qualities, even sounds and similar things become extremely distant realities. It's simply impossible for inanimate objects like clothing to experience happiness or other feelings.

831

If all things exist inherently in their causes, then wouldn't analysis of things be pointless? And if your causes are simply happiness and similar states, then blankets and other such things could never arise from them.

832

Comfort and similar feelings arise from things like blankets and cushions. When these things are absent, such feelings of comfort do not exist. Yet these feelings of comfort and the like are never experienced as something permanent.

833

If happiness and other feelings are clearly present, why aren't they perceived? If these feelings become subtle, how can they be both coarse and subtle at the same time?

834

When the coarse dissolves and becomes subtle, This very process of change from coarse to subtle shows impermanence. In this same way, why not accept That all things are impermanent?

835

If physical pleasure is nothing more than pleasure itself, then pleasure would be clear and impermanent. If you claim that "nothing can arise from what doesn't exist, because nonexistence cannot produce anything," then consider this: even though you don't accept that clarity can arise from nothing, the fact remains that it does exist.

836

If the result already existed within the cause, then eating food would mean eating excrement. If you buy cotton seeds with money intended for cloth, you might as well wear those seeds.

837

If the confused world does not see the truth, let it be—it is still the truth regardless.

838

This knowledge exists in the ordinary world too, so why isn't it recognized? If ordinary perception isn't reliable, then even seeing something clearly isn't necessarily true.

839

If valid cognition itself is not valid, wouldn't that make whatever it measures false? In that case, the meditation on emptiness would not be reliable.

840

Without first grasping conceptual existence, one cannot grasp non-existence. Therefore, whatever illusory existence appears, Its non-existence is clearly just as illusory.

841

Therefore, when dreaming that your child has died, the thought "they are gone" is an obstacle to the thought "they are here." Yet both of these thoughts are equally false.

842

Therefore, when examined thoroughly in this way, nothing exists without a cause. Nor does anything abide in individual causes or in their collection.

843

Things do not truly come from anywhere, stay anywhere, or go anywhere. What the confused mind takes to be real—how is it any different from an illusion?

844

Examine where an illusion comes from, Where things created by causes originate, Where they come from, And where they go.

845

When something appears only when another thing is nearby, and doesn't appear when that thing is absent—like a reflection—how can it be truly real?

846

If something already exists, what need is there for a cause? And if something doesn't exist, what purpose would a cause serve?

847

Even with millions of causes, that which does not exist cannot be transformed into existence. How can a temporary state be truly real? And what else could be changed into something real?

848

If nothing exists, then existence is impossible. So when could anything ever come to exist? Without things first being born into existence, non-existence can never be separated from them.

849

If something is never without non-existence, then it cannot possibly exist. Yet it cannot become non-existent either, since this would mean it has two different natures.

850

Thus, there is no cessation, And no real things exist either. Therefore, all these beings Have always been unborn and never ceased.

851

Living beings are like a dream. When examined deeply, they are like plantain trees - hollow inside. Whether in the state beyond suffering or not, In ultimate reality, there is no difference.

852

When all things are empty of inherent existence, what is there to gain or lose? Who is there to honor you or disrespect you?

853

Where does happiness come from, and what causes suffering? What is truly pleasant, and what is truly unpleasant? If you search for their ultimate nature, what is it that desires, and what is being desired?

854

When we examine this world of the living, who exactly within it will not die? What is yet to occur will surely happen, and what has already happened is done. Who truly remains a lasting relative or friend?

855

May I embrace everything, seeing it all as similar to empty space. Those who wish for their own happiness create causes for conflict and excitement.

856

In our world, people live with great difficulty, caught up in agitation and momentary pleasures, experiencing grief, struggling with constant efforts, engaging in conflicts, harming and hurting one another, and sustaining themselves through harmful actions.

857

After coming to the higher realms again and again, And experiencing many pleasures, When you die, you fall into the unbearable And long-lasting sufferings of the lower realms.

858

In the world, there are many dangerous pitfalls. This kind of ultimate truth cannot be found there. And because of all the contradictions that exist, No such perfect truth can be found in worldly existence.

859

Even in this life, we face an endless ocean of immeasurable and unbearable suffering. Here we have so little strength, and our time is so very short.

860

Even in that place, life is wasted on: Being healthy but doing nothing meaningful, Exhausting yourself just to satisfy hunger, Sleeping, causing harm to others, And pointlessly spending time with foolish people.

861

Life passes quickly without meaning, And deep understanding is extremely hard to find. Once habits of distraction take hold, How can they ever be reversed?

862

Even on the spiritual path, there is danger of falling into great suffering, as negative forces work diligently to lead you astray. Many wrong paths appear along the way, and doubts arise that are difficult to overcome.

863

This precious human life is extremely hard to find again, And encountering a Buddha's teachings is even more rare. The river of disturbing emotions is so difficult to overcome— Alas, suffering will continue endlessly.

864

Even though they suffer terribly, these beings don't recognize their own suffering. Living in this river of misery, they deserve our compassion—how sad that they remain unaware of their plight!

865

It's like someone who repeatedly bathes And then repeatedly jumps into fire. Despite being in tremendous suffering, They still imagine themselves to be happy.

866

If you live as though old age and death do not exist, acting carelessly in what you do, death will catch you by surprise. Then you'll fall into the unbearable suffering of the lower realms. When you live as if aging and dying will never happen, behaving recklessly in your activities, death will suddenly strike you down first—and then you'll experience the intolerable anguish of rebirth in the lower realms.

867

When will I become one who cools Those beings tormented by suffering's flames? When will I bring the refreshing rains of happiness Born from my own clouds of merit?

868

When one respectfully gathers merit in a way that is free from fixation, then one becomes able to teach the nature of emptiness to those who are harmed by their fixations.

869

From "Entering the Conduct of Bodhisattvas," this is the ninth chapter on the Perfection of Wisdom.

870

Through whatever goodness I have created By composing this Guide to the Bodhisattva's Way of Life, May all beings enter into The path of awakened conduct.

871

In all directions, may those who suffer physically and mentally, All who are afflicted by pain and illness, Through my positive actions, Find an ocean of happiness and joy.

872

May beings never be separated from happiness for as long as they remain in the cycle of existence. May all living creatures continuously experience the uninterrupted joy that bodhisattvas possess.

873

May all living beings in every hell realm throughout the universe find joy through the happiness and bliss of Sukhavati (the Pure Land of Great Bliss).

874

May those suffering from cold find warmth. From the great clouds of bodhisattvas, May endless rivers of water flow forth To cool those suffering from heat.

875

May forests of razor-sharp blades transform into dense sandalwood groves. May trees with piercing thorns become wish-fulfilling trees.

876

May the hellish regions be transformed into delightful places, beautified by the sweet calls of blue jays, ducks, geese, and other birds singing their melodious songs. May these realms be filled with fragrant lotus-covered lakes, turning places of suffering into lands of joy and pleasure.

877

May heaps of coal be transformed into piles of precious jewels. May scorched earth be covered with crystal floors. May mountains crushed together become magnificent offering palaces filled with Buddhas.

878

From now on, may the burning coals, scalding stones, and rain of weapons All be transformed into a shower of flowers. And may those who attack each other with weapons Now throw flowers at one another in playful delight.

879

Those who have sunk into the pit of fire-like rivers of hell, Whose flesh has decayed and whose bones are white as jasmine flowers— Through the power of my virtue, may they obtain divine bodies And dwell with goddesses beside gently flowing streams.

880

Why do the messengers of death, crows, and fearsome vultures here suddenly become terrified? Who has the wonderful power to dispel all darkness and create happiness and joy? When I look up, I see Vajrapani blazing brilliantly in the midst of the sky. Filled with intense joy, may I be freed from all negative actions and accompany him.

881

May a rain of flowers and scented water fall down and instantly extinguish the blazing coals of hell. May those in hell feel sudden joy and wonder, "What is happening?" And may they look up to see Avalokiteshvara, the One Who Holds the Lotus.

882

Friends, abandon your fears and come quickly here to me! A powerful youth with a blazing topknot has arrived—one who eliminates all fear. Through his power, all suffering vanishes and the force of joy prevails. Compassion and the awakening mind have arisen in him, completely protecting all beings.

883

Hundreds of gods bow their crowned heads to your lotus feet. Your compassionate eyes are moist as flower petals rain down upon your head. A thousand beautiful goddesses sing your praises from their celestial mansions, saying "Look at this!" Upon seeing Manjushri in such splendor, may those in the mental hells now cry out in joy.

884

Through my virtuous roots, may all beings in hell realms experience great joy when they see bodhisattvas like Samantabhadra, who are like clouds without obscurations, bringing down cool, fragrant, pleasant rain.

885

May all animals be free from the fear of being eaten by one another. May hungry spirits find happiness, just like the humans of the Northern Continent.

886

May the stream of milk flowing from the hand of noble Avalokiteshvara satisfy all hungry ghosts, and through bathing in it, may they always be refreshed and cooled.

887

May the blind regain their sight, and may the deaf always hear sounds. May pregnant women give birth safely and without harm, just like the goddess Maya.

888

May those who are naked find clothing, May those who are hungry find food, May those who are thirsty find water, And may they obtain delicious drinks.

889

May the poor find wealth. May those who are suffering and distressed find joy. May the despondent be uplifted and encouraged. And may everyone attain perfect stability and well-being.

890

May all beings who are sick Be quickly freed from their illness. May all diseases that afflict living beings Permanently cease to exist.

891

May those who are afraid become fearless. May those who are bound be set free. May those without power gain strength. And may all beings' hearts grow gentle toward one another.

892

May all travelers find ease and comfort in every direction they go. May the purposes of their journeys be accomplished without difficulty or hardship.

893

May those who travel by boat or ship Accomplish their heart's desires. After reaching the shore safely, May they rejoice together with their loved ones.

894

May those who wander lost on sorrowful paths Meet with fellow travelers on the way. May they journey onward without weariness, with ease, Free from the dangers of thieves, tigers, and other threats.

895

May the gods protect those who are vulnerable: Children and the elderly who are without guardians, Those who have fallen asleep or become intoxicated, When they are in desolate places, dangerous paths, and frightening areas without routes.

896

May I be free from all exhaustion and fatigue, Filled with faith, wisdom, and compassion. With perfect nourishment and conduct, May I always remember my past lives.

897

May everyone enjoy endless resources like the treasury of space. May they use these freely, without conflict or harm to others.

898

May all beings of little presence become magnificent in their splendor. May those who practice austerities with unattractive forms be blessed with perfect, beautiful appearances.

899

May all women in the world Be transformed into men. May those who are lowly attain high status, And may their pride be overcome.

900

Through this merit that I have created, May all beings without exception Abandon all harmful actions And always engage in what is good.

901

May I never be separated from the awakening mind, may I devote myself to the conduct of enlightenment, may I be completely embraced by the buddhas, and may I abandon all harmful actions.

902

May all these living beings Live for immeasurable lifespans. May they always thrive in happiness, And never even hear the word "death."

903

May every direction be filled with pleasure gardens of wish-fulfilling trees, where Buddhas and their spiritual heirs proclaim the beautiful sound of the Dharma.

904

Everywhere, may the ground become clean and free from debris and stones. May it be smooth like the palm of a hand, with the gentle, polished nature of lapis lazuli.

905

Throughout all the gathering circles, wherever they may be, may many bodhisattvas dwell here, adorning this world with their own goodness.

906

May all living beings hear the sound of Dharma Continuously flowing from birds and trees, From every ray of light, and even from the sky itself.

907

May they always meet with the Buddhas and the Buddha's spiritual children. May they make boundless offerings, like clouds of gifts, to these supreme teachers of all beings.

908

May the divine beings send rain at the right time. May the harvests be abundant and plentiful. May the rulers govern according to the dharma. And may the world prosper and flourish.

909

May medicines be potent and effective. May mantras recited in practice be accomplished. May dakinis, spirits, and other beings Be filled with compassionate hearts.

910

May no being suffer, become afraid, or be objects of contempt. May no one experience mental distress.

911

May monasteries be filled with the sounds of reading and recitation. May these sacred places flourish through the practice of study. May the community of practitioners always remain in harmony, And may the goals of the spiritual community be fulfilled.

912

May monks who wish to practice the teachings find solitude. Having abandoned all distractions, may they cultivate a pliant and workable mind.

913

May the nuns be well-supported and free from quarrels and harm. Likewise, may all monastics maintain their ethical discipline without decline.

914

May I grow weary of immoral behavior and continuously work to exhaust all negative actions. May I also attain a fortunate rebirth, and even there, may my commitment to ethical discipline never decline.

915

May I honor the wise and receive even the simplest offerings. May my mind become completely pure, And may my reputation spread in all directions.

916

May they never experience the suffering of the lower realms. May they, without enduring hardships, Swiftly attain enlightenment With bodies even more excellent than those of the gods.

917

May all beings make offerings to all buddhas again and again, and through the immeasurable happiness of the buddhas, may they always experience joy and well-being.

918

May bodhisattvas accomplish their heartfelt intention to benefit all beings. May all living beings receive whatever the compassionate protectors have wished for them.

919

May the solitary realizers and the hearers also be happy.

920

Through the kindness of Manjushri, May I remember all my past lives And maintain the monastic discipline Until I reach the first level of great joy.

921

May I enjoy simple food and necessities while maintaining good health. In all my future lives, may I find perfect conditions to practice in solitary places.

922

When I wish to see you or have even a small question, may I encounter you, Manjushri, my protector, without any obstacles.

923

In order to benefit all sentient beings throughout space in all directions, may my actions become just like those of Manjushri.

924

As long as space exists, And as long as living beings remain, May I too remain, To dispel the suffering of all beings.

925

May all the suffering of living beings, whatever it may be, ripen upon me instead. And through the goodness of the community of bodhisattvas, may all beings enjoy happiness.

926

May this teaching—the sole medicine for the suffering of all beings and the source of all happiness—remain for a long time, along with its resources and the honor it deserves.

927

I bow to Manjushri, through whose kindness virtuous thoughts arise. I also bow to my spiritual friend, through whose kindness I flourish.
