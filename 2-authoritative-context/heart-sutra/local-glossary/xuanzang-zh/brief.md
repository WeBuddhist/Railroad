# Translation Brief — Xuanzang 玄奘 (649 CE)

## About This Translation

Xuanzang (602–664 CE) was the most celebrated Buddhist translator in Chinese history, renowned for his pilgrimage to India and his systematic rendering of Sanskrit texts into Chinese. His translation of the Heart Sutra (649 CE) is the version that became canonical in East Asian Buddhism — it is the text chanted daily in Chinese, Japanese, Korean, and Vietnamese Buddhist traditions. Its brevity (260 Chinese characters) and rhythmic density made it ideal for liturgical use.

This translation corresponds structurally to the **shorter Sanskrit recension**: it lacks the narrative frame (setting at Vulture Peak, Buddha's samādhi, Śāriputra's dialogue) and the closing frame (Buddha's approval, assembly's joy) that define the longer recension. It begins directly with Avalokiteśvara's practice and ends with the mantra, producing a self-contained doctrinal core without narrative scaffolding.

## Style Profile

- **Literalness**: moderate — translates content accurately but with compression and occasional interpretive additions
- **Technical terms**: translated into Chinese equivalents rather than transliterated, except for the mantra
- **Register**: formal classical Chinese (文言文); terse, epigrammatic
- **Audience assumption**: practitioners familiar with basic Buddhist concepts; no explanatory notes
- **Verse rendering**: continuous prose in numbered sections
- **Structural completeness**: shorter recension only — no narrative frame, no closing

## Translation Philosophy

Xuanzang consistently preferred semantic translation over phonetic transliteration for doctrinal terms, reversing the earlier Chinese convention favoured by Kumārajīva. His rendering of the Heart Sutra reflects this: *prajñāpāramitā* becomes 般若波羅蜜多 (a semi-transliteration retained from earlier tradition), while *anuttarā samyaksaṁbodhi* is phonetically rendered 阿耨多羅三藐三菩提. The mantra alone is given as pure transliteration.

The extreme conciseness of this translation — removing the dialogue frame, compressing negation lists, and eliminating several propositions present in the Sanskrit — suggests Xuanzang was working from a source closer to the shorter Sanskrit recension and optimising for liturgical memorability.

## Relationship to Source Text

### Recension alignment

Xuanzang's translation follows the **shorter Sanskrit recension** as its structural model but contains several passages that align with the **longer Sanskrit recension** rather than the shorter. The translation cannot be explained by either Sanskrit recension alone.

Passages aligning with the **longer Sanskrit recension** (against the shorter):

- **Dhātu enumeration** (^1-4): Xuanzang ends at 無意識界 (*manovijñānadhātu*, 18th dhātu). The shorter Sanskrit ends at *manodhātu* (17th); the longer Sanskrit explicitly includes *manovijñānadhātuḥ*. Xuanzang follows the longer recension here.
- **Bodhisattva's dwelling** (^1-7): Xuanzang opens this section with 以無所得故 ("because of non-attainment"), corresponding to *aprāptitvena* in the longer Sanskrit. This word is absent from the shorter Sanskrit. Xuanzang follows the longer recension here.

Passages where Xuanzang **diverges from both Sanskrit recensions**:

- **^1-1**: Xuanzang adds 度一切苦厄 ("crossed beyond all suffering and difficulty"). Neither Sanskrit recension has an equivalent. This is a unique doctrinal addition.
- **^1-3**: Xuanzang renders *anūnā asaṁpūrṇāḥ* ("not deficient, not complete") as 不增不減 ("not increasing, not decreasing"). This is a different conceptual pair — quantitative change rather than ontological completeness. The same divergence appears in Facheng, suggesting a shared source variant.
- **^1-5**: Xuanzang negates only *avidyā* (無無明), omitting the *vidyā* negation present in both Sanskrit recensions. Shared with Facheng — likely a common source variant rather than an independent error.
- **^1-7**: Xuanzang adds 夢想 ("dream-thoughts") with no Sanskrit equivalent in either recension. Not in Facheng. Unique to Xuanzang.
- **^1-9**: The opening mantra epithet is rendered 大神咒 ("great divine mantra"). Neither Sanskrit recension supports 神 ("divine/spirit"). The corresponding Sanskrit is *prajñāpāramitāmantro* (longer) or *prajñāpāramitāmahāmantro* (shorter). Xuanzang reads in a sense of divine efficacy not in the source text.

Passages where Xuanzang **omits material from the longer Sanskrit**:

- **^1-2**: The third form/emptiness proposition (*yad rūpaṁ sā śūnyatā yā śūnyatā tad rūpam*) is absent. Shared gap with Facheng.
- **^1-6**: The longer Sanskrit negates both *prāpti* and *aprāpti* (*na prāptir nāprāptiḥ*). Xuanzang has only 無得 (no attainment), omitting the negation of non-attainment. Facheng preserves this; Xuanzang alone drops it.
- **Full narrative and closing frames**: absent entirely, consistent with the shorter recension's structure.

### Summary verdict

Xuanzang's source text was neither the extant shorter nor the extant longer Sanskrit recension, but something structurally similar to the shorter (no narrative frame) with several doctrinal passages matching the longer. The two shared gaps with Facheng (^1-3 and ^1-5) suggest a common Chinese transmission that already differed from both extant Sanskrit recensions at these points. The unique additions (度一切苦厄, 夢想) and the unique omission (*nāprāptiḥ*) are specific to Xuanzang's version.
