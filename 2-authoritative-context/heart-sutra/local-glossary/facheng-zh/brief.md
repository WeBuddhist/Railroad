# Translation Brief — Facheng 法成 (9th century CE)

## About This Translation

Facheng (Tibetan: Chos grub, fl. 820–848 CE) was a Chinese monk working at Dunhuang who translated texts in both directions — from Chinese into Tibetan and from Tibetan and Sanskrit into Chinese. His translation of the Heart Sutra is preserved in the Dunhuang cave manuscripts (Dunhuang manuscript P.2178 and related sources), which were sealed around 1000 CE and rediscovered in 1900. This gives it a distinct transmission history from the Xuanzang version, which circulated in the Chinese canonical mainstream.

Facheng's translation corresponds to the **longer Sanskrit recension**: it preserves the full narrative frame (setting at Vulture Peak, Buddha's samādhi, Śāriputra's question and Avalokiteśvara's reply, closing with Buddha's approval and the assembly's joy). It is one of only two Chinese translations — alongside the earlier Fachang (法炬, attributed) rendering — that preserves the dialogue structure found in the longer Sanskrit and Tibetan canonical versions.

## Style Profile

- **Literalness**: high — structurally complete, tracks the Sanskrit closely section by section
- **Technical terms**: translated into Chinese equivalents rather than transliterated, with a preference for semantic clarity over liturgical concision
- **Register**: formal classical Chinese (文言文); more expansive than Xuanzang
- **Audience assumption**: monastic scholars with access to the full text; not optimised for liturgical memorability
- **Structural completeness**: longer recension — full narrative frame and closing preserved
- **Length**: approximately double Xuanzang's 260 characters, owing to the preserved frames

## Translation Philosophy

Facheng worked in a multilingual scholarly environment at Dunhuang and had direct access to Tibetan translations and Tibetan commentarial traditions, in addition to Sanskrit sources. His translation decisions show a consistent preference for semantic rendering of technical terms over phonetic transliteration (e.g. 無上正等菩提 for *anuttarā samyaksaṁbodhi* versus Xuanzang's 阿耨多羅三藐三菩提). This tendency toward translation over transliteration makes his version more immediately comprehensible to readers without Sanskrit background.

Where Xuanzang compresses, Facheng expands — compare 心無罣礙 (Xuanzang) with 心無障礙 (Facheng) for *cittāvaraṇanāstitvā*: both are accurate, but Facheng's 障礙 ("obstruction/hindrance") maps the Sanskrit etymology more directly, while Xuanzang's 罣礙 ("entanglement") is more idiomatic.

## Relationship to Source Text

### Recension alignment

Facheng's translation follows the **longer Sanskrit recension** throughout. It is the strongest Chinese correspondence to the longer recension in the Heart Sutra folder, preserving structural and doctrinal content that Xuanzang omits.

Passages where Facheng **uniquely matches the longer Sanskrit** (against Xuanzang):

- **Full narrative frame** (^2-1 through ^2-6): 如是我聞 opening, setting at Vulture Peak, Buddha's samādhi (甚深明了三摩地), Śāriputra prompted by Buddha's power (承佛威力), the full dialogue with Avalokiteśvara. All absent from Xuanzang, all present in the longer Sanskrit.
- **^2-13 — *nāprāptiḥ***: Facheng has 無得亦無不得 ("no attainment and also no non-attainment"). This corresponds to *na prāptir nāprāptiḥ* in the longer Sanskrit. Xuanzang has only 無得 (no attainment) — the negation of non-attainment is absent from Xuanzang but present in both Facheng and the longer Sanskrit.
- **^2-14 — *aprāptitvena***: Facheng has 以無所得故 ("because of non-attainment"), matching *aprāptitvena* in the longer Sanskrit. Xuanzang also has this, but it is absent from the shorter Sanskrit — so both Chinese translators follow the longer recension here.
- **Full closing frame** (^2-18 through ^2-22): Śāriputra instruction summary, Buddha's approval (善哉善哉), the tathāgatas' rejoicing (一切如來亦當隨喜), assembly's joy (皆大歡喜). All present in the longer Sanskrit, entirely absent from Xuanzang.

Passages where Facheng **diverges from both Sanskrit recensions**:

- **^2-3**: Facheng renders *anūnā asaṁpūrṇāḥ* as 無減無增 ("no decrease, no increase"). This is the same conceptual substitution as Xuanzang's 不增不減 — quantitative change rather than ontological completeness. Both Chinese translators independently produce a different formulation from either Sanskrit recension. Likely a common source variant. ⚑
- **^2-12**: Facheng negates only *avidyā* (無無明), omitting the *vidyā* negation present in both Sanskrit recensions. Shared gap with Xuanzang — likely a common transmission variant rather than an independent error. ⚑
- **^2-7**: The third form/emptiness proposition (*yad rūpaṁ sā śūnyatā yā śūnyatā tad rūpam*) is absent. Shared gap with Xuanzang.
- **^2-16**: The opening mantra epithet is rendered 大密咒 ("great secret mantra"). Neither Sanskrit recension supports 密 ("secret"). Xuanzang renders the same epithet as 大神咒 ("great divine mantra"). Both Chinese translators add an interpretive qualifier absent from the Sanskrit. ⚑
- **^2-22**: Facheng adds 信受奉行 ("received in faith and practiced accordingly") as a closing formula. No Sanskrit equivalent in either recension. This is a standard Chinese Buddhist sūtra closing formula inserted by convention.

Passages where Facheng is **more precise than Xuanzang**:

- **^2-9 — *amalā vimalā***: Facheng 無垢離垢 maps the two Sanskrit terms closely — 無垢 = *amalā* (without stain), 離垢 = *vimalā* (free from stain). Xuanzang's 不垢不淨 ("not defiled, not pure") substitutes a different conceptual pair. Facheng is closer to the Sanskrit here.
- **^2-13 — *nāprāptiḥ***: See above — Facheng alone preserves this negation.
- **^2-14 — no 夢想**: Facheng does not add 夢想 ("dream-thoughts"). Xuanzang's addition of 夢想 (no Sanskrit equivalent) is absent from Facheng, which follows the Sanskrit without this insertion.
- **^2-15 — *samyaksaṁbodhi***: Facheng translates semantically as 無上正等菩提. Xuanzang phonetically transliterates as 阿耨多羅三藐三菩提. Facheng's rendering is more transparent.

### Summary verdict

Facheng's translation is a substantially complete rendering of the longer Sanskrit recension. The structural correspondence is strong throughout, and several doctrinal details (especially *nāprāptiḥ* and the narrative frames) confirm the longer recension as Facheng's source. The two shared gaps with Xuanzang (^2-9 and ^2-12) suggest a common Chinese transmission variant that differed from both extant Sanskrit recensions at these points. Facheng's link to `root-text-sk.md` (the longer recension) is the most well-supported correspondence in the Heart Sutra folder.
