## Gloss Divergences

- ⚑ **དྲི་མ་དང་བྲལ་བ་མེད་པ vs དྲི་མ་དང་བྲལ་བ**: The source text reads 'དྲི་མ་དང་བྲལ་བ་མེད་པ' (not undefiled), which is a double negation. The Sanskrit source 'विमला' (vimalā) means 'undefiled' or 'pure'. The primary reading in the gloss above corrects the Tibetan to 'དྲི་མ་དང་བྲལ་བ' (undefiled) to match the Sanskrit. Many Tibetan commentaries interpret the original Tibetan as 'not truly pure' in an ultimate sense, but the Sanskrit is a direct positive assertion of purity.

## Standardized text

ཤཱ་རིའི་བུ་དེ་ལྟ་བས་ན་ཆོས་ཐམས་ཅད་སྟོང་པ་ཉིད་དེ། མཚན་ཉིད་མེད་པ། མ་སྐྱེས་པ། ​མ་འགགས་པ། དྲི་མ་མེད་པ། དྲི་མ་དང་བྲལ་བ། ​བྲི་བ་མེད་པ། གང་བ་མེད་པའོ། །

A significant semantic variant was corrected from 'དྲི་མ་དང་བྲལ་བ་མེད་པ' (not undefiled) to 'དྲི་མ་དང་བྲལ་བ' (undefiled/pure). This correction aligns the Tibetan text with the authoritative Sanskrit reading 'विमला' (vimalā), which means 'undefiled' or 'pure', resolving a direct negation difference. The original Tibetan reading, meaning 'not undefiled', contradicted the Sanskrit's positive assertion of purity. This correction maintains the metrical structure of the verse.

## Term analysis

### `ཤཱ་རིའི་བུ་ (shA ri'i bu)`

The term 'ཤཱ་རིའི་བུ་' (Shāri'i bu) matches the Sanskrit 'शारिपुत्र' (śāriputra) and is consistently used across all Tibetan commentaries, indicating no meaning-altering discrepancy.

### `དེ་ལྟ་བས་ན་ (de lta bas na)`

The term 'དེ་ལྟ་བས་ན་' (de lta bas na, therefore/thus) aligns with the Sanskrit 'एवं' (evaṃ, thus). While some Tibetan commentaries present stylistic variants like 'དེ་ལྟར་' (de ltar) or 'དེ་ལྟར་ན་' (de ltar na), these are common synonyms or grammatical expansions in Tibetan and do not alter the core meaning. No meaning-altering discrepancy is found.

### `ཆོས་ཐམས་ཅད་ (chos thams cad)`

The phrase 'ཆོས་ཐམས་ཅད་' (chos thams cad, all phenomena) perfectly matches the Sanskrit 'सर्वधर्माः' (sarvadharmāḥ) and is consistently used in all Tibetan commentaries. No meaning-altering discrepancy is found.

### `སྟོང་པ་ཉིད་དེ། (stong pa nyid de)`

The term 'སྟོང་པ་ཉིད་' (stong pa nyid, emptiness) corresponds to 'शून्यता' (śūnyatā) within the Sanskrit compound 'शून्यतालक्षणा'. The meaning of emptiness is consistent, and this is not considered a meaning-altering discrepancy.

### `མཚན་ཉིད་མེད་པ། (mtshan nyid med pa)`

The Tibetan 'མཚན་ཉིད་མེད་པ' (mtshan nyid med pa, without characteristics) is a widely accepted reading in the Tibetan tradition, supported by numerous commentaries. While the provided Sanskrit has 'शून्यतालक्षणा' (śūnyatālakṣaṇā), meaning 'characterized by emptiness', this can be interpreted within the Mahayana context as 'having the characteristic of being empty, i.e., lacking inherent characteristics'. Many other Sanskrit versions of the Heart Sutra explicitly list 'अलक्षणा' (alakṣaṇā, without characteristics) after 'शून्यता'. Given the strong Tibetan tradition and the contextual flow of negations, this is considered a valid semantic interpretation rather than a meaning-altering discrepancy.

### `མ་སྐྱེས་པ། (ma skyes pa)`

The term 'མ་སྐྱེས་པ' (ma skyes pa, unproduced) directly matches the Sanskrit 'अनुत्पन्ना' (anutpannā) and is consistent across all Tibetan commentaries. No meaning-altering discrepancy is found.

### `མ་འགགས་པ། (ma 'gags pa)`

The term 'མ་འགགས་པ' (ma 'gags pa, unceased) directly matches the Sanskrit 'अनिरुद्धा' (aniruddhā) and is consistent across all Tibetan commentaries. No meaning-altering discrepancy is found.

### `དྲི་མ་མེད་པ། (dri ma med pa)`

The term 'དྲི་མ་མེད་པ' (dri ma med pa, undefiled) directly matches the Sanskrit 'अमला' (amalā) and is consistent across all Tibetan commentaries. No meaning-altering discrepancy is found.

### `དྲི་མ་དང་བྲལ་བ་མེད་པ། (dri ma dang bral ba med pa)` (discrepancy)

The Tibetan phrase 'དྲི་མ་དང་བྲལ་བ་མེད་པ' (dri ma dang bral ba med pa) translates to 'not undefiled' or 'not pure' due to the double negation. This is a direct semantic contradiction to the Sanskrit 'विमला' (vimalā), which means 'undefiled' or 'pure'. This constitutes a meaning-altering textual variant, as it reverses the intended quality from purity to its negation. The Sanskrit is the highest authority here.

### `བྲི་བ་མེད་པ། (bri ba med pa)`

The term 'བྲི་བ་མེད་པ' (bri ba med pa, undiminished) directly matches the Sanskrit 'अनूना' (anūnā) and is consistent across all Tibetan commentaries. No meaning-altering discrepancy is found.

### `གང་བ་མེད་པའོ། ། (gang ba med pa'o)`

The term 'གང་བ་མེད་པའོ' (gang ba med pa'o, not full) directly matches the Sanskrit 'असंपूर्णाः' (asampūrṇāḥ) and is consistent across all Tibetan commentaries. No meaning-altering discrepancy is found.
